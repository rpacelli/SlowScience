Rethinking Scientific Production

The Slow Science Movement advocates for a more thoughtful, sustainable approach to research that values quality over quantity and depth over speed.

Read Our Manifesto

About the Movement

The Slow Science Movement challenges the prevailing "publish or perish" culture in academia and research institutions. We believe that the current pressure for rapid publication and high citation counts undermines the quality, integrity, and societal value of scientific work.

Our movement advocates for a shift to more thorough research practices that allow scientists the time needed for deep thinking, careful literature reveiw, and meaningful collaboration.

We envision a scientific ecosystem where researchers are evaluated based on the significance and rigor of their contributions rather than biblometric indices.

The Slow Science Manifesto

some scientists
July 2025

üì• Download Full Manifesto (PDF)

The modern research system, driven by the relentless mantra of "Publish or Perish" has become unsustainable. Efficiency, once a virtue, has turned into a vice, fostering an anti-scientific climate of hypercompetition, stress, and short-term thinking. This system sacrifices the very essence of science: the pursuit of truth, the joy of discovery, and the ethical responsibility to society.

We thus proclaim the need for a new Slow Science revolution--a call to reclaim the values of curiosity, collaboration, and deep thinking in scientific research.

The Current Situation

Global production of peer-reviewed science and engineering articles climbed from 2.0 million in 2010 to 3.3 million in 2022 - a 65% jump in just twelve years; output still grows about 4% every year. Nonetheless, there are indications that the character of scientific progress is shifting away from immediate, paradigm-shattering disruptions and toward consolidation, specialization, and incremental improvement. This unsustainable growth of publications comes at a great cost, both for researchers and for science. Some examples are listed in the following.

  1. Human Cost. Researchers are often forced to sacrifice family, friendships, and personal well-being, with no limits on working hours. This high pressure environment is exacerbated by the systemic precarity itself, as a constant stream of fixed-term researchers feel compelled to work day and night to produce enough for the next grant or the next short-term contract, just to remain in the system. In this context, burnout is normalized, and work-life balance is treated as a luxury for the fortunate few who achieve stability, rather than a fundamental right.
  2. Competition Over Collaboration. The pressure to outperform peers stifles meaningful cooperation, both within and across disciplines. Yet science thrives on shared knowledge, not secrecy.
  3. Truth Suppressed. The race for high-impact publications incentivizes deception: negative results are hidden, methods are cherry-picked, and reproducibility suffers. Science should be built on honesty, not hype.
  4. Metric manipulation and fraud. The pressure to publish incentivizes outright misconduct. There are some clear indications that researchers increasingly engage in excessive self-citation and manipulate bibliographic networks, while some now exploit the peer-review process itself. A recent case involved a professor embedding hidden text, readable only by AI-powered review systems, to artificially inflate the paper's perceived quality and secure positive evaluations. This move from "citation gaming" to "review gaming" marks a dangerous new frontier where the very mechanisms of quality control are being systematically corrupted.
  5. Fashion Over Substance. Young researchers are pushed into trendy fields where citations are abundant, rather than pursuing original, risky, or fundamental questions.
  6. Innovation Penalized. High-risk, high-reward research is abandoned in favor of safe, incremental work. True breakthroughs require time--yet time is a luxury few can afford.
  7. Exclusion & Inequality. Speed favors those who can work without interruption. Early-career researchers, people with health issues, and especially women (who still bear disproportionate caregiving burdens) are systematically disadvantaged, worsening the gender gap.
  8. Teaching & Outreach Undervalued. Mentoring, public engagement and education--essential for a healthy scientific culture--are dismissed as distractions, since hiring and promotions reward only publications.
  9. Intellectual costs. Information overload makes it impossible to track progress outside a narrow niche; whole lines of inquiry are periodically rediscovered because prior work is lost in the flood. The pace leaves no space for deep study, quiet reflection or learning a new discipline, fostering self-reinforcing, doctrinaire schools of thought.

The AI Precipe: A Case Study in Systemic Failure

The field of Artificial Intelligence epitomizes several of these dysfunctions. It is increasingly defined by a culture of hyper-competition that rewards incremental progress at the expense of transformative breakthroughs. This trend is amplified by a significant migration of researchers from diverse fields, who are often drawn more by the availability of funding and the topic's high visibility than by deep scientific curiosity. The resulting influx intensifies competition, fostering an environment where the primary objective shifts toward producing minor, low-risk results on "hot-topic" problems to ensure rapid publication. This dynamic inherently marginalizes high-risk, foundational research. Exacerbating the issue, the peer-review process frequently reinforces this narrow focus. The system tends to favor papers that use the latest popular tools and datasets. This creates a narrow path for what is considered "successful" research, often confined to tech industry giants possessing the computational resources needed to fuel this kind of research. In doing so, work that offers new, fundamental ideas is overlooked and new approaches are unfairly penalized. This environment not only stifles genuine innovation but also diverts talent and resources towards short-term trends, acting as a powerful drain on the broader scientific ecosystem.

What We Demand

From funders:

We reject winner-takes-all grants that concentrate resources in the hands of few that gain disproportionate advantages. We ask for broad, steady funding enabling many teams to explore risky ideas over long horizons.

From institutions:

We reject promotion rubrics that equate merit with publication counts. We ask for holistic evaluation of quality, openness, mentoring, teaching and outreach.

From publishers:

We reject volume-driven business models. We ask for incentives for replication, negative results and slow, careful review.

From scientists:

We reject the racing to the next incremental paper. We ask for collaborative, interdisciplinary research and open sharing of failures as well as successes.
